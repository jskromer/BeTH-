Editorial Note — On the "Deficit"

Throughout this newsletter, Taplin frames Berkeley's fiscal situation as a crisis driven by rising costs and insufficient revenue. BeTH's analysis of Berkeley's ACFR and AAO records tells a different story: the projected $29M shortfall is largely a product of chronic over-budgeting — the systematic practice of appropriating far more than is ever spent, rolling unspent funds forward through the AAO process, and obscuring true fiscal capacity. A "deficit" built on spending plans that never materialize is not the same as a structural funding gap. Readers should evaluate Taplin's budget claims in that context.

Budget & Fiscal Claims
⚠ Partially Confirmed Berkeley faces a $29 million/year budget shortfall over the next two fiscal years.

The $29M figure is real and appears in city budget documents and news coverage. However, the framing of this as a revenue or cost crisis misses the underlying mechanism: Berkeley has consistently budgeted far more than it spends. BeTH's AAO tracking shows over $915M in mid-cycle amendments across FY2024–FY2026, with hundreds of millions in unspent appropriations recycled annually. The city's own ACFR shows General Fund expenditures regularly coming in well under budget. A projected shortfall built on ambitious spending plans is fundamentally different from a cash shortfall.

The $29M gap is confirmed as the city's stated figure — but its cause is budget process dysfunction, not structural insolvency.

Public Safety & Crime Statistics
⚠ Partially Confirmed Shootings dropped 40%, and for the first time in at least a decade Berkeley recorded zero fatal or injury shootings.

Total shootings fell from 25 in 2024 to 15 in 2025 — a confirmed 40% decline, hitting a nine-year low. However, the claim of "zero fatal or injury shootings" omits an important fact: there was one police-involved shooting that resulted in injury. The newsletter's claim appears to refer specifically to non-police-involved shootings. The "first time in at least a decade" framing is consistent with the available data going back to ~2017, but the police shooting is a meaningful caveat that goes unmentioned.

⚠ Partially Confirmed Robberies fell 20% from 221 to 177.

The 20% decline is confirmed across multiple sources. The exact figures (221 → 177) match BPD's full-year annual report. Year-end media reports using preliminary data cited slightly different numbers (223 → 179), likely due to different reporting cutoff dates. The direction, magnitude, and approximate figures are well-supported.

⚠ Partially Confirmed Motor vehicle thefts declined 48% from 1,093 to 572.

A dramatic ~48–50% decline in motor vehicle thefts is confirmed across all sources. The specific baseline and endpoint figures vary by source (Berkeleyside reports 1,058 → 536; the Berkeley Scanner reports 1,048 → 528 using year-to-date data). The newsletter's figures of 1,093 → 572 are consistent with the BPD annual report and are not refuted, but differ slightly from preliminary media reports. The magnitude is accurate.

⚠ Partially Confirmed ALPR program: 58 arrests, 121 investigations. Flex Team: 141 arrests, 23 operations. Combined: 199 arrests.

The ALPR/Flock figures (58 arrests, 121+ investigative leads) are confirmed by BPD's council presentation and widely reported in coverage of the Flock contract renewal debate. The 199 combined total depends on both components being accurate; only the ALPR half is externally verified.

✓ Confirmed BPD operating at 154 officers vs. authorized 174, down from 181 before last budget cycle.

Confirmed. BPD ended 2025 with 154 sworn officers, 20 short of its authorized 174. The prior authorized ceiling of 181 was reduced as a budget measure. Note: more recent reporting (April 2026) suggests active staffing has continued to fall (~137 officers with ~15 on modified duty), meaning current conditions may be even more strained than the newsletter's figures suggest.

Regional ALPR / Surveillance Claims
✓ Confirmed Richmond turned off ALPR cameras in November 2025; vehicle theft jumped 33%; council voted to bring them back.

Thoroughly confirmed. Richmond Police Chief Timothy Simmons deactivated the Flock system on November 19, 2025. Vehicle thefts increased approximately 33% in the months following (172 vs. 129 in the same prior-year period). On March 17–18, 2026, the Richmond City Council voted 4-3 to reinstate the Flock contract through end of 2026. All three elements of this claim are well-documented.

✓ Confirmed Oakland approved a $2.25M two-year ALPR contract in December 2025.

The Oakland City Council voted 7-1 on December 17, 2025 to approve a $2.25M, two-year contract with Flock Safety for approximately 290 ALPR cameras citywide. The amount, term, city, and timing are all confirmed.

✓ Confirmed — with context San José City Council voted unanimously to keep its 474-camera network.

Confirmed, but the framing is incomplete. The San José City Council voted unanimously on March 10, 2026 to retain its 474-camera Flock network. However, the vote came with significant new restrictions: data retention cut from one year to 30 days, cameras prohibited near reproductive health clinics and places of worship, and new authentication requirements added. Taplin frames this as a simple endorsement of the status quo; it was actually a conditional continuation with meaningful constraints — a distinction that matters for Berkeley's own policy debate.
