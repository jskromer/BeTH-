History of the Habit

Berkeley's fiscal dysfunction is not a crisis. It's a culture — documented across five decades of audits, ignored warnings, bait-and-switch ballot measures, and one-time fixes that never fix anything.

Research compiled April 2026  ·  Sources linked throughout
Every five to ten years, Berkeley declares a budget emergency, cuts services, goes to voters for a new tax, and promises to address the "structural" problem. Then it doesn't. The next cycle begins. This page documents the pattern — in the city's own words, through its own auditors, using its own records.

Pre-1978 The Era of Constraint
Before 1978
Berkeley runs on property taxes
Before Proposition 13, Berkeley's city budget was primarily funded by property taxes — a stable, predictable revenue source. The General Fund was constrained by actual revenues. Budget process workarounds were limited because they were unnecessary: revenues covered obligations without creative accounting. This era represents Berkeley's last period of genuine fiscal discipline.
1978 — The Inflection Point Proposition 13 Breaks the Model
June 1978
Prop 13 passes — Berkeley loses 53% of property tax revenue overnight
Revenue Shock Berkeley voted against Prop 13 by nearly 3-to-1. It passed statewide anyway. The city's primary revenue source collapsed. Within days, a bitterly contested 5–4 council majority used the emergency to strip community agencies of funding. The city hastily approved a "catch-all package" of tax and revenue increases on June 30 — one day before the initiative took effect. The workaround culture was born.
1980
Library Relief Act — Template #1: Take it off the General Fund
Bait & Switch Berkeley passes the Library Relief Act, a square-footage parcel tax that takes the public library entirely out of the General Fund. Sold as "saving the library." Effect: freed General Fund money for other uses with no accountability to voters about how it would be spent. This becomes the template for every subsequent dedicated tax measure.
1986
BSEP established — the pattern scales to schools
Bait & Switch The Berkeley Schools Excellence Program parcel tax is created, taking school funding out of the General Fund with a dedicated tax. BSEP passes every election cycle since. Each renewal reinforces the political formula: promise a specific service, collect a dedicated tax, manage the General Fund separately with minimal accountability.
2001–2006 Dot-Com Bust: First Repeat of the Cycle
Late 2003
$9.5M deficit — fire parcel tax proposed, then pulled in embarrassment
Bait & Switch Berkeley faces an $8–10M General Fund deficit with projections of $19.5M within five years. Mayor Tom Bates proposes a parcel tax titled "Special Tax to Fund Fire Prevention and Protection Services." Problem: only $2 million of the $7 million measure was actually going to fire services. The Berkeley Fire Fighters Local 1227 declares opposition to their own namesake tax. Bates withdraws the measure 24 hours before the council vote. Voters are saved from funding a shell game — this time.
2005
Auditor issues "Delayed Implementation" report — recommendations ignored since 1998
Audit Ignored City Auditor Ann-Marie Hogan formally documents that audit recommendations are going unimplemented — some dating back to 1998. This is the first formal record of Berkeley's systematic pattern of ignoring its own auditor. By 2012, 23 recommendations from 10 separate audits will still be outstanding.
2006–2012 Great Recession Cycle: The Warning Is Written Down and Ignored
2006 / 2008
City Manager puts the answer in writing — twice. Nobody acts.
Audit Ignored City Manager Phil Kamlarz states in the 2008–2009 Budget Book cover letter — citing a 2006 warning:
"The only method to effectively eliminate the City's structural deficit is through cost reductions — primarily through controlling labor costs, since employee salary and benefits make up 77% of the City's operating budget." — City Manager Phil Kamlarz, 2008 Budget Book (citing 2006 report)
This sentence will still be quoted in op-eds in 2016, unchanged, because nothing changed.
January 2009
$8.5M state takeaway — and an 8% raise for the City Manager
Fiscal Contradiction Staff discloses potential $8.5M in state takeaways. Contingency fund already depleted. Hiring freezes, cuts, and overtime restrictions proposed. Simultaneously, Mayor Bates proposes an 8% salary increase for City Manager Kamlarz — from $214,836 to $232,020 — while the Budget Manager advises the council to "proceed with caution."
March 2010
$14.6M deficit — "most serious downturn" in 35 years of service
City Manager Kamlarz, with 35 years at Berkeley, calls it the worst he's seen. Transfer tax revenue collapses from $16M to $8M. 67 positions proposed for elimination. Refuse collection rates already up 20% the prior year. This is the third consecutive "unprecedented" fiscal crisis in 12 years.
November 2010
Auditor: "Employee Benefits — Tough Decisions Ahead" — $250M+ in unfunded liabilities
PensionAudit Ignored City Auditor Ann-Marie Hogan releases a landmark report:
  • $250M+ in unfunded pension and benefit liabilities (actuarial basis)
  • Using actual asset values: $400M+ unfunded
  • Berkeley pays not just employer CalPERS contributions but most employees' own 8% contribution
  • Sick-leave cash-outs at retirement flagged as unusual and costly — former City Manager Kamlarz receives $147,439 on departure, plus $20,785/month pension ($250K/year total)
Auditor recommends eliminating sick-leave cash-out policy. Compliance requested by September 2012. It is not implemented.
November 2011
"Failing Streets" audit — $54M backlog, 32x cost multiplier for delay
Audit IgnoredInfrastructure Auditor Hogan finds over 62% of Berkeley's 216 centerline miles of streets need resurfacing or reconstruction. Cost: $54 million. Key finding: the cost of reconstructing a failed street is 32 times the cost of timely maintenance. The report wins a national auditing award. The streets continue to fail. By 2019, the backlog reaches $251M. By 2024, $286M for streets alone. By 2025, total infrastructure backlog: $1.8 billion.
February 2012
Pension unfunded liability: $329.87M — council told, council ignores
Pension Police safety pension contributions require 42% of salary. Full funding in 20 years would require 50.7% — or 61.5% if funded in 10 years. UC Berkeley economist Tim Wallace of Berkeley Budget SOS group: "The city auditor had warned the City Council they were in real trouble but the council paid no attention."
2013–2018 The Long Drift: Same Problems, Bigger Numbers
2013
Pensions and infrastructure named as "key budget liabilities" — again
Berkeleyside reports pensions and infrastructure as Berkeley's primary budget challenges — the same two issues identified in 2010, 2011, and 2012. Third consecutive year of public identification with no corrective action.
June 2016
"Berkeley's Fiscal Condition is Not Good. Really" — $1B in unfunded liabilities
PensionInfrastructure Finance analyst Barbara Gilbert publishes a comprehensive op-ed:
  • ~$1 billion in unfunded liabilities — half employees, half infrastructure — "with no meaningful way to pay these down"
  • Berkeley's bond rating is AA+ not AAA because it lacks "a formal policy and plan to address unfunded pension liabilities"
  • Most recent labor negotiations "worsened the situation dramatically"
  • She quotes Kamlarz's 2006/2008 warning directly: "Here we are in 2016."
Source: Berkeleyside, June 2016 →
November 2016
Measure U1 — "affordable housing" tax goes to General Fund, spent on Council chambers
Bait & Switch Voters approve Measure U1 (75–25%) — a business license tax increase on landlords, sold as funding for affordable housing. The city deliberately structures the measure so revenues flow to the General Fund, not the Housing Trust Fund — making the promise legally unenforceable. Within one year: $6.65M from U1 revenues spent to purchase the former Premier Cru building for new City Council chambers. Berkeleyside headline: "Berkeley has done a bait and switch by using housing funds to buy new Council chambers." By 2025: $6.1M redirected to close General Fund gap; $2.5M cut from Small Sites affordable housing program.
"Berkeley has done a bait and switch by using housing funds to buy new Council chambers." — Berkeleyside opinion, March 31, 2017
March 2018
Pension unfunded liability hits $640M — up $500M in a decade
Pension Analysis by Isabelle Gaston: Berkeley owes $640M+ in unfunded pension obligations, including ~$100M in retiree healthcare to which employees contribute nothing during employment. CalPERS assumed 7.0%+ returns; actual 10-year return: 4.4% — during one of the strongest bull markets in history. City officials continue to say they are "working on it."
May 2018
Auditor Ann-Marie Hogan retires after 24 years — leaving behind $600M+ in unfunded liabilities
Hogan, elected in 1994, issued hundreds of audit reports. She leaves behind a city with crumbling infrastructure, an unfunded pension crisis, and a pattern of audit non-implementation she documented herself in 2005. Her successor, Jenny Wong, will find the same problems — plus new ones.
2019–2022 COVID Covers the Cracks
November 2020
Measure FF fire tax — oversight bypassed within months of passage
Bait & SwitchOversight Bypassed Voters approve Measure FF, an $8.5M/year parcel tax for fire services with mandatory citizen oversight. Within months:
  • 9 retired firefighters hired for vegetation management in September 2020 — commission not informed until December
  • $2M Outdoor Public Warning System purchased without consulting the commission
  • $40M training facility proposed without budget review
  • Commission given PowerPoints instead of budget documents
Commission Chair Jose Luis Bedolla: "The commission continues to ask for transparency and clarity."
May 2022
Auditor Wong: finances "strong for now" — but $773M pension liability, $1.2B infrastructure backlog
PensionInfrastructure New City Auditor Jenny Wong's first major financial condition audit covers FY 2012–FY 2021. Pension and OPEB obligations: $773.1M (up from $567.4M in 2012). Infrastructure backlog: $1.2B. California State Auditor rates Berkeley's pension funded ratio (66%) as "high risk." Berkeley has not met its own targets for pension contributions.
November 2022
Measure L — $650M infrastructure bond fails. Voters don't trust the city to spend it.
Voter Rejection Berkeley's largest-ever proposed bond measure — $650M for streets, housing, wildfire safety, infrastructure — receives 59.4% yes, short of the required 66.7%. City leaders refused to commit revenues to specific projects. Result: the $1.2B (now $1.8B) backlog has no identified funding mechanism. The pattern: ask voters for money with no accountability strings. Voters say no.
2023–2026 The Current "Crisis" — Same Script, New Cast
January 2025
2009 lease audit reopened: "Berkeley Faces the Same Risks 16 Years Later"
Audit Ignored City Auditor Jenny Wong reopens the 2009 lease management audit. Findings: the city still lacks a clear approach to lease management, a relevant policy, and a complete inventory of leases and licenses. The city had lost track of an entire building. 7 of the 24 original recommendations were dropped in 2022 without resolution; 8 are now re-opened. Total span of non-implementation: 23 years and counting.
February 2026
$43M in no-bid contracts — 94 contracts, no documented competitive process
Governance Failure Auditor Wong finds 94 contracts worth $43M in FY 2024 entered without documented competitive bidding. In FY 2022, nearly $85M in long-term recycling contracts awarded without competition. No evidence of corruption — but elevated risk of overpaying and zero quality assurance.
April 8, 2026
Auditor Wong: "Structural Deficit Poses Risk to Financial Sustainability"
Fund SweepPensionOne-Time Fixes The most comprehensive audit to date — FY 2016 through FY 2025:

  • "The City relied on one-time funding sources to balance its budget for the past several years"

April 2026
The $29M "crisis" — and the familiar solution
Bait & Switch The city's proposed response to the current deficit: 38 filled position layoffs, elimination of 100 vacant positions, and a 0.5% sales tax on the November 2026 ballot — projected to raise $9M/year — with the implicit threat that Fire Station 4 closes if voters say no. Budget Manager Sharon Friedrichsen acknowledges: "A lot of these options are not sustainable." This is the same playbook used in 2003, 2009, and 2010. The names have changed. The script has not.

The Recurring Playbook

Six tools, used in rotating combination across five decades:

1. Over-Budget & Pocket the Savings

Personnel lines are systematically over-budgeted. The "savings" are booked as mid-year windfalls, recycled through AAO amendments with minimal public scrutiny. The FY 2025 mid-year update explicitly attributes savings to "over budgeting for program managers." In FY 2021 alone, unencumbered carryovers totaled $35.4M.

2. Raid Internal Service Funds

When the General Fund runs short, Berkeley sweeps surpluses from internal funds — workers' compensation, parking, marina. In FY 2025: $4.7M from workers' comp despite that fund being $2M short of covering its own claims. The auditor warned this puts long-term fund sustainability at risk. It happened anyway.

3. Ballot Measure "Bait & Switch"

Tax measures are framed around specific popular services (libraries, fire, schools, affordable housing) but structured to flow to the General Fund, leaving the council free to spend proceeds however it chooses. When challenged, the legal disclaimer is: "We never legally promised anything." Voters approved it anyway.

4. Defer Maintenance, Declare Emergency Later

The 2011 "Failing Streets" audit found a $54M backlog and warned that delay multiplies costs 32-fold. Berkeley deferred. By 2025 the backlog is $1.8B — a 33x increase in 14 years. The same dynamic applies to pools, piers, facilities, and pension contributions. Deferral is free today and catastrophic later.

5. Ignore the Auditor

As of 2026: 65 open recommendations across 9 audits. Some date to 1998. The lease management audit was first issued in 2009 — the city had lost track of an entire building by 2025, when the same audit was reopened with the same findings. The audit function exists. The implementation function does not.

6. Threaten Cuts to Essential Services

Each cycle ends with the same political ultimatum: approve new revenue or fire stations close, police are cut, libraries shorten hours. In 2026 it's Fire Station 4. In 2010 it was 67 positions. In 2003 it was fire services (while redirecting most of the tax elsewhere). The service threat is the lever. It has worked repeatedly.
